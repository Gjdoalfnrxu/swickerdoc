Abstract

Stock-Flow Consistent (SFC) macroeconomic models have attracted growing attention as a theoretically coherent alternative to Dynamic Stochastic General Equilibrium frameworks, particularly in the wake of the 2008–2009 Global Financial Crisis and the subsequent reassessment of mainstream macroeconomic methodology. However, SFC models have been subject to limited systematic empirical validation against historical national accounts data, and virtually no cross-country comparative validation exercises have been published for the core Godley-Lavoie model family.

This paper presents a multi-country empirical validation framework for the SIMEX model — a five-sector open-economy extension of the Godley-Lavoie SIM prototype — applied to five OECD economies: New Zealand, Australia, Canada, the United Kingdom, and the United States. The framework calibrates country-specific model parameters using Ordinary Least Squares regression and accounting-ratio methods over the pre-COVID estimation window of 2010–2019, then evaluates model fit against the full 2010–2023 historical period using standard time-series metrics: Mean Absolute Percentage Error (MAPE), Pearson correlation coefficient, and mean bias.

Countries were selected to span a range of structural characteristics — from highly open commodity-exporting economies (New Zealand, Australia, Canada) to large semi-closed economies (United States) and service-led open economies (United Kingdom) — allowing evaluation of model performance under varying import propensity, capital intensity, and fiscal structure. Validation targets primary national income accounting aggregates: gross domestic product (Y), household consumption (C), imports (M), and tax revenue (T). Known structural limitations of the current model specification — in particular the absence of an investment accelerator and a supply-side production function — are documented and excluded from the pass-fail assessment accordingly.

Results are forthcoming upon completion of multi-country data assembly and framework implementation. This paper documents the research design, theoretical basis, and analytical methodology in advance of results, in the interest of pre-registration and methodological transparency. Upon completion, the paper will constitute, to the authors' knowledge, the first systematic empirical validation of the Godley-Lavoie SIM/SIMEX model family across multiple OECD national accounts datasets.

1. Introduction

The 2008 Global Financial Crisis exposed significant gaps in the predictive capacity of mainstream macroeconomic models. Dynamic Stochastic General Equilibrium (DSGE) models, the dominant workhorse of central bank and academic macroeconomics through the 2000s, characteristically failed to anticipate the financial instability that preceded the crisis and, in many cases, assigned negligible probability to the output collapses that followed it (Caballero, 2010; Wren-Lewis, 2012). Post-crisis reassessment opened intellectual space for alternative modelling traditions, among which the Stock-Flow Consistent approach — associated principally with Wynne Godley and Marc Lavoie — has gained notable traction.

The SFC framework's foundational appeal is its accounting rigour. Every financial asset in an SFC model has a corresponding liability; every flow in a period accumulates into the stock variables of the next period by construction. The balance sheet matrix and transaction-flow matrix are both explicit and exhaustive: no sector can run a surplus without another running a deficit, and no financial instrument can appear on one sector's assets without appearing symmetrically on another's liabilities. This accounting coherence — trivially true in a national accounts framework but routinely violated in reduced-form econometric models and DSGE approximations — provides a structural guarantee that no "black holes" exist in the system (Godley and Lavoie, 2007, p.6).

Despite the theoretical appeal and the growing body of SFC modelling literature, a significant empirical gap exists. The overwhelming majority of published SFC work operates in simulation mode: constructing stylised model economies with calibrated or assumed parameters and using simulation to demonstrate theoretical mechanisms — the paradox of thrift, the financial instability hypothesis, sectoral balance dynamics. Far fewer papers attempt to validate SFC models against actual historical national accounts data for specific economies, and fewer still do so across multiple countries with systematic metric-based evaluation. This stands in contrast to the empirical calibration and validation tradition in DSGE modelling, where empirical fit is an expected component of any published model.

The gap matters for practical reasons. SFC models are increasingly used to inform policy analysis — including in New Zealand's context, where the Reserve Bank has published exploratory SFC work on financial stability — and model credibility is appropriately conditioned on empirical performance. Without systematic validation, it is difficult to assess whether parameter choices are reasonable, whether the model tracks the main cyclical features of the economy, or where structural extensions are most urgently required.

This paper makes the following contributions. First, it introduces a replicable multi-country calibration and validation framework for the SIMEX model — a five-sector open-economy SFC model built on the Godley-Lavoie SIM prototype — applied to five structurally diverse OECD economies. Second, it provides a pre-registered methodology including explicit pass-fail thresholds, defined estimation and evaluation windows, and documented exclusions. Third, it advances the methodology of SFC model validation by distinguishing structural model limitations (which should not be counted against the model in assessment) from calibration failures (which should). The distinction between I = depreciation as a structural constraint versus a miscalibrated consumption propensity as a fixable error is conceptually important and has not, to the authors' knowledge, been formalised in prior SFC validation work.

The remainder of this paper is organised as follows. Section 2 presents the theoretical framework, including the SIM model equations, the SIMEX five-sector extension, and the balance sheet matrices. Section 3 describes the data, country selection criteria, calibration methodology, validation metrics, and software framework architecture. Section 4 presents results (currently pending). Section 5 discusses findings in light of model theory and known limitations. Section 6 concludes.

2. Theoretical Framework
2.1 The SIM Model

The point of departure for the SIMEX model family is the SIM model, presented as the first complete worked example in Godley and Lavoie (2007, Chapter 3). SIM is a closed, three-sector economy consisting of Households, Firms, and Government. Despite its simplicity, SIM exhibits the essential stock-flow accounting closure that distinguishes the SFC approach: all injections and leakages balance within the period, and the resulting stock accumulation dynamics are explicitly tracked.

The core behavioural and accounting equations of SIM are as follows. National income (Y) is determined by aggregate demand:

(1) Y = C + G

where C denotes household consumption expenditure and G denotes government expenditure, taken as exogenous. Tax revenue T is a constant proportion of national income:

(2) T = θ · Y

where θ ∈ (0,1) is the average effective tax rate. Household disposable income YD is defined as post-tax income:

(3) YD = Y − T

The household consumption function is the Keynesian-Kaldorian specification with a wealth effect, following Godley and Lavoie (2007, pp.71–74):

(4) C = α₁ · YD + α₂ · Hh[−1]

where α₁ is the marginal propensity to consume out of current disposable income, α₂ is the marginal propensity to consume out of the beginning-of-period household wealth stock Hh, and [−1] denotes the one-period lag. The parameters satisfy α₁, α₂ > 0 and α₁ + α₂ < 1 as a stability condition (Godley and Lavoie, 2007, p.73). The household wealth accumulation identity follows directly:

(5) Hh = Hh[−1] + YD − C

The government's budget constraint requires that any fiscal deficit (G > T) is financed by the issuance of high-powered money Hg:

(6) ΔHg = G − T

and the stock-flow consistency condition — that the sum of all sector financial balances equals zero — is satisfied by construction. In SIM, the only financial asset is government money H, so ΔHh + ΔHg = 0 must hold (Godley and Lavoie, 2007, p.67). This identity closes the model.

SIM admits a closed-form steady-state solution. Setting ΔHh = 0 (no change in wealth), equations (3)–(5) yield a unique steady-state income level Y* = G/θ, the standard balanced-budget multiplier result. The consumption function (4) ensures that the economy converges to this steady state from any initial position, provided the stability condition holds. The speed of convergence is governed by α₂: lower α₂ implies slower adjustment and a longer transient path (Godley and Lavoie, 2007, pp.78–80).

2.2 The SIMEX Extension: A Five-Sector Open Economy

The SIMEX model generalises SIM in two directions: it opens the economy to international trade and financial flows (adding a Rest of World sector), and it introduces an explicit banking sector (adding a Banks sector and a Central Bank sector). The result is a five-sector model — Households (HH), Firms (F), Government (G), Banks (B), and Central Bank / Reserve Bank (CB) — with correspondingly richer balance sheet and transaction-flow matrices.

The open economy extension follows the OLOS model of Godley and Lavoie (2007, Chapter 6). Exports X are treated as exogenous, representing world demand for the domestic economy's output. Imports M are endogenously determined by a fixed import propensity μ applied to national income:

(7) M = μ · Y

The national income identity becomes:

(8) Y = C + I + G + X − M

where investment I in the current model specification (SIMEX v4) is set equal to replacement depreciation:

(9) I = δ · K[−1]

where δ is the depreciation rate and K is the capital stock. This specification implies K = K[−1] + I − δ · K[−1] = K[−1], a fixed capital stock. This is a known structural limitation of the current model version — investment exhibits no cyclical sensitivity to output gaps or interest rates — and is documented as Discrepancy 2 in the model's development roadmap. Investment is therefore excluded from the pass-fail validation assessment.

The Net Foreign Asset position NFA evolves according to the current account balance:

(10) NFA = NFA[−1] + X − M

The banking sector introduces credit money. Firms borrow from banks to finance investment; households hold deposits at banks. The lending rate rl is set as a spread over the central bank's policy rate rbar:

(11) rl = rbar + spread

Deposit interest income supplements household income, and loan interest expense reduces firm income (and, by extension, aggregate demand). The central bank sets rbar exogenously — in the empirical application, this is calibrated to each country's observed policy rate path (OCR in New Zealand, Cash Rate in Australia, Bank Rate in the United Kingdom, Fed Funds Rate in the United States, and Overnight Rate in Canada).

Household consumption in SIMEX uses the same functional form as SIM (equation 4), with Hh now representing household deposits V, incorporating both the income effect α₁ and the wealth effect α₂. The full household sector balance includes interest receipts on deposits and, in later model versions, mortgage interest payments that reduce effective disposable income.

2.3 Balance Sheet Matrix

A key feature of SFC models is the explicit balance sheet matrix, which maps all financial assets and liabilities across sectors. For SIMEX, the aggregate balance sheet is as follows (stylised; positive = asset, negative = liability):

Item Households Firms Banks Government Rest of World Sum
Capital (K) +K +K
Deposits (V) +V −D 0
Loans (L) −L +L 0
High-powered money (H) +Hh +Hb −H 0
Net Foreign Assets (NFA) −NFA 0
Bank net worth (NWb) NWb NWb
Net worth V + Hh K − L NWb −H −NFA K

The sum of all financial asset-liability pairs is zero by construction. The only net wealth in the system is the physical capital stock K, consistent with the standard SFC accounting result (Godley and Lavoie, 2007, p.30). The stock-flow consistency condition requires that this balance sheet closes in every period, and the simulation engine enforces this condition algebraically through the Gauss-Seidel iterative solver.

3. Data and Methodology
3.1 Country Selection

Five OECD economies are selected for the validation exercise. The selection criteria prioritise: (i) data availability and quality over the 2010–2023 period in standardised international datasets; (ii) structural diversity across key model parameters, particularly import propensity μ, capital-to-output ratio K/Y, and tax revenue share θ; and (iii) methodological tractability — economies for which the SIMEX model family's assumptions are broadly applicable.

Country Code Economy type Approx. μ Approx. K/Y Rationale
New Zealand NZ Small open, commodity exporter 0.26–0.28 ~2.2 Baseline economy; initial calibration developed here
Australia AU Medium open, commodity exporter 0.21–0.23 ~2.5 Same data sources as NZ; structural peer for cross-validation
Canada CA Medium open, commodity exporter 0.30–0.34 ~3.0 Mid-sized open economy; commodity export price volatility stresses X equation
United Kingdom UK Large open, service-led 0.28–0.32 ~2.8 Higher θ (~0.36) and post-Brexit trade structure change test model robustness
United States US Large, semi-closed 0.12–0.15 ~3.5 Low import propensity and deep capital markets test model with closed-economy dynamics

This selection spans the principal dimensions of structural variation relevant to the SIMEX model: import propensity (from approximately 0.14 for the United States to 0.32 for the United Kingdom and Canada), capital intensity (from K/Y ≈ 2.2 for New Zealand to K/Y ≈ 3.5 for the United States), and fiscal structure (effective tax rates from approximately 0.25 for the United States to approximately 0.36 for the United Kingdom). A sixth candidate — South Korea — is explicitly reserved for a subsequent phase, as the current model specification cannot adequately capture South Korea's investment-led growth dynamics (I/Y ≈ 0.30–0.32) without an investment accelerator.

3.2 Data Sources

All primary macroeconomic flow variables — gross domestic product (Y), household consumption (C), gross fixed capital formation (I), government final consumption and investment (G), exports (X), and imports (M) — are sourced from the World Bank World Development Indicators (WDI) database, using constant national currency series to avoid exchange rate confounding. The WDI indicator codes used are: NY.GDP.MKTP.KN, NE.CON.PRVT.KN, NE.GDI.FTOT.KN, NE.CON.GOVT.KN + NE.GDI.GOVT.KN, NE.EXP.GNFS.KN, and NE.IMP.GNFS.KN. Government expenditure G is constructed as the sum of government final consumption and government gross fixed capital formation.

Tax revenue T is sourced from the IMF World Economic Outlook database (series GGR_NGDP, general government revenue as a share of GDP) multiplied by nominal GDP, then deflated to real terms using each country's own GDP deflator. Where IMF WEO coverage is incomplete, OECD Revenue Statistics (total tax receipts, category 1100) is used as a substitute. GDP deflators are sourced from WDI (NY.GDP.DEFL.ZS), rebased to a country-specific base year of 2019 = 1.0.

The household wealth proxy V — used in equation (4) as the lagged wealth variable — is constructed from M2 broad money supply, sourced from national central bank publications: the Reserve Bank of New Zealand (RBNZ M2), the Reserve Bank of Australia (RBA Statistical Table B2), the Bank of England (BoE Series M4), the Federal Reserve (FRED H.6 release), and the Bank of Canada. This is an acknowledged proxy limitation: M2 does not capture housing equity, pension assets, or direct equity holdings, all of which are economically significant components of household net worth in each of these economies. The implications of this measurement choice are discussed in Section 5.

Policy interest rates (rbar) are sourced from the Bank for International Settlements (BIS) policy rate dataset and supplemented by direct central bank time series: the RBNZ Official Cash Rate, the RBA Cash Rate Target, the BoC Overnight Rate, the Bank Rate (United Kingdom), and the Federal Funds Rate (United States). Lending and deposit rates are sourced from the BIS Statistics Table F.1.1 and central bank statistical releases, used to calibrate the interest rate spread parameter.

Capital stock data are sourced from the IMF Investment and Capital Stock Dataset (series kpriv_r and kgov_r, converted to constant 2011 USD and then to constant local currency using purchasing power parity adjustments). Where IMF capital stock data is unavailable for specific country-years, the perpetual inventory method is applied using the gross fixed capital formation series with country-specific depreciation rates.

All monetary values are expressed in constant local currency units and converted to model units (MU) using a country-specific scaling factor. The scaling factor is chosen such that GDP at the 2019 base year falls between 50 and 65 MU, a range that ensures numerical stability in the Gauss-Seidel solver without loss of economic interpretability. The scaling factors applied are: NZD 5 billion per MU (New Zealand), AUD 30 billion per MU (Australia), CAD 40 billion per MU (Canada), GBP 40 billion per MU (United Kingdom), and USD 400 billion per MU (United States). Crucially, parameter values (θ, μ, α₁, α₂) are scale-invariant ratios and are unaffected by this choice.

3.3 Calibration Methodology

Model parameters are estimated from historical data over the pre-COVID calibration window of 2010–2019. This window is selected to exclude the COVID-19 period (2020–2022) from parameter estimation, on the grounds that supply-side shocks, extraordinary fiscal transfers, and unconventional monetary policy during this period represent structural breaks incompatible with the model's demand-driven framework. Parameters estimated over 2010–2019 are then applied to the full 2010–2023 window for validation, with 2020–2023 serving as an implicit out-of-sample test period.

The effective tax rate θ is estimated as the mean ratio of real tax revenue to real GDP over the calibration window:

θ = (1/T) · Σt=20102019 (Tt / Yt)

This ratio method is preferred over OLS regression because tax revenue is structurally proportional to income by construction in the model, and allowing an intercept would introduce a constant fiscal balance term inconsistent with the model equations.

The import propensity μ is estimated analogously as the mean import-to-GDP ratio:

μ = (1/T) · Σt=20102019 (Mt / Yt)

The consumption parameters α₁ and α₂ are estimated by OLS regression of household consumption C on current disposable income YD and the one-period lag of household wealth V[−1]:

Ct = α₁ · YDt + α₂ · Vt−1 + εt

The regression is estimated without an intercept, consistent with the theoretical consumption function. The stability condition α₁ + α₂ < 1 is enforced as a post-estimation constraint: if OLS returns parameter values violating this condition, theory-consistent defaults of α₁ = 0.75 and α₂ = 0.08 are substituted with a flagged warning. Expected ranges, informed by prior empirical work and the New Zealand prototype calibration, are α₁ ∈ [0.65, 0.85] and α₂ ∈ [0.05, 0.12].

The depreciation rate δ is estimated from the IMF capital stock data as the mean ratio of consumption of fixed capital to lagged capital stock over the calibration window, or equivalently from the perpetual inventory identity δ ≈ (K[−1] + I − K) / K[−1]. Expected ranges are δ ∈ [0.06, 0.12].

Initial conditions for stock variables (K, V, L, D, NFA, NWb) at the start of the validation window (2010) are set directly from observed data where available, converted to model units. This is a critical design choice: the SIMEX simulation engine supports an initial_conditions override in the run payload that sets stock variables at the commencement of period 1, bypassing the model-definition initial values. Without this capability, all validation would be confounded by model-scale mismatches — a problem documented extensively in the New Zealand prototype work, where the absence of initial condition overrides produced a six-fold scale discrepancy and MAPE values exceeding 200% for all variables.

3.4 Validation Metrics

Model fit is assessed using three primary metrics for each target variable (Y, C, M, T):

Mean Absolute Percentage Error (MAPE) measures level accuracy as the average absolute percentage deviation of model output from observed data:

MAPE = (100/n) · Σt |( ŷt − yt) / yt|

Pearson correlation coefficient (r) measures trend tracking — the degree to which the model reproduces the directional movements in each variable over time, independent of level:

r = Cov(ŷ, y) / [SD(ŷ) · SD(y)]

Mean bias is calculated as the mean signed percentage deviation, providing a directional measure of systematic over- or under-prediction:

Bias = (100/n) · Σt (ŷt − yt) / yt

Pass-fail thresholds are set ex ante as follows:

Metric Pass Warn Fail
Pearson r ≥ 0.85 0.70 – 0.85 < 0.70
MAPE ≤ 20% 20% – 35% > 35%
Mean bias (%) ≤ 10% 10% – 20% > 20%
SFC identity (Σ sector balances) = 0 (tol. 1e−6) — ≠ 0

A country receives an overall PASS if all of Y, C, M, and T pass all three metrics. A WARN is assigned if any variable falls in the Warn band and none in the Fail band. A FAIL is assigned if any variable fails any metric. The SFC identity is treated as a hard pass-fail condition: a non-zero sectoral balance sum indicates a stock-flow inconsistency in the simulation, which invalidates the run regardless of other metric performance.

Investment (I), capital stock (K), and household wealth (V) are excluded from pass-fail assessment but are reported descriptively. Investment is excluded because the current model constrains I = δK, producing a constant series by construction; a flat series cannot be validly assessed against a cyclical empirical series. Capital stock and wealth are excluded because both are highly sensitive to initial condition calibration and proxy measurement choices, making cross-country comparison of these metrics unreliable at the current stage of framework development.

Metrics are reported for two sub-periods: the pre-COVID window (2010–2019) and the full sample (2010–2023). Divergence between these two windows provides a qualitative indication of model performance under the structural breaks of the COVID-19 period.

3.5 Validation Framework Architecture

The validation framework is implemented as a Python software package (sfc-tool/validation/framework/) with a modular architecture organised around five core components.

The API client (api_client.py) provides a thin wrapper over the SIMEX REST API, abstracting the mechanics of model registration (POST /models), run initiation with initial condition overrides (POST /runs), and result retrieval (GET /runs/{id}/results). Authentication is via JWT bearer token passed through environment variables, avoiding hardcoded credentials in the codebase.

The data loader (data_loader.py) reads per-country CSV files in a standardised format, applies the country-specific GDP deflator to convert nominal to real values, and scales all variables to model units using the country-specific MU size. Currency normalisation is handled entirely within the loader, so downstream components operate exclusively in deflated model-unit terms.

The calibrator (calibrator.py) implements the parameter estimation procedures described in Section 3.3. OLS estimation for the consumption function uses numpy.linalg.lstsq. The calibrator returns a country-specific parameter dictionary and validates that all estimated parameters satisfy theoretical constraints before writing a JSON configuration file.

The shock builder (shock_builder.py) constructs the time-indexed shock arrays required by the SIMEX API from the historical data series. Government expenditure G and exports X are supplied as level-change shocks relative to their baseline values. The policy rate rbar is supplied as a period-by-period level shock. Endogenous variables (C, Y, M, T) are not shocked — they are model outputs, not inputs.

The metrics engine (metrics.py) computes MAPE, Pearson r, and mean bias for each target variable, evaluates pass-fail status against the thresholds defined in Section 3.4, and generates per-country and combined summary reports. The reporter (reporter.py) renders these outputs as Markdown reports and as a combined HTML summary table. The plotter (plotter.py) generates six-panel charts comparing model versus actual trajectories for Y, C, I, M, T, and the NFA position.

The entry point run_multi_validation.py orchestrates the full pipeline: load configurations, calibrate parameters, register models, submit runs, collect results, compute metrics, and write reports. Individual country runs are accessible as pytest test files (test_{country}_validation.py), allowing integration with continuous integration pipelines.

5. Discussion
5.1 Expected Cross-Country Variation in Model Performance

Model theory generates testable prior expectations about differential performance across the five economies. These priors are stated here before results are observed, consistent with the pre-registration spirit of this paper.

Import propensity and GDP tracking. In the SIMEX framework, GDP is determined by the national income identity (equation 8). For economies with high import propensity μ, a larger share of any demand increase leaks abroad, moderating the income multiplier. This means that calibration errors in μ have a larger absolute effect on Y for open economies (New Zealand, Canada, United Kingdom) than for the United States. The hypothesis, prior to results, is that Y MAPE will be larger for small open economies than for the United States — not because the model is structurally less appropriate, but because the import propensity acts as a multiplier on any calibration error in G or X.

Capital intensity and initial conditions. Economies with higher K/Y ratios (United States at K/Y ≈ 3.5) require correspondingly larger absolute capital stock initial conditions. Measurement error in K0 — which is non-trivial given the heterogeneous capital stock methodologies across national statistics offices — propagates through the depreciation equation (9) and affects the level of investment and, in future model versions, the investment-output dynamics. This suggests that K and V validation metrics will be noisier for capital-intensive economies, although the exclusion of I from the pass-fail assessment partially insulates the overall results.

Tax rate stability and θ calibration. The United Kingdom has the highest effective tax rate in the sample (θ ≈ 0.36), reflecting the comprehensive scope of public services. The flat-rate tax specification (equation 2) may be a poorer approximation for the UK than for New Zealand or Australia, where the effective rate is lower and shows less cyclical variation. The hypothesis is that T MAPE will be higher for the United Kingdom than for the commodity-exporting economies.

COVID period performance. The COVID structural break (2020–2022) involved supply-side production losses, extraordinary government transfers unrelated to tax revenue, and a collapse in consumer confidence — none of which are captured by the SIMEX demand-driven framework. All five economies experienced simultaneous output contractions in 2020 that cannot be replicated by exogenous G and X shocks alone, since the supply-side channel (firms unable to produce) is absent from the model. Full-sample MAPE values are therefore expected to exceed pre-COVID values for all countries, with the magnitude of degradation reflecting each economy's COVID response intensity and depth of contraction.

5.2 Structural Limitations and Their Bearing on Results

The interpretation of validation results must be conditioned on known structural limitations of the current model specification. These are not calibration failures — they are properties of the model version being validated — and conflating them with poor fit would be methodologically incorrect.

Investment dynamics (I = δK). The absence of an investment accelerator means that investment neither tracks the business cycle nor responds to interest rate changes. This is the single largest structural limitation for the validation exercise. In all five economies, gross fixed capital formation is procyclical — falling in recessions and rising in expansions — and is sensitive to both output expectations and financing costs. The flat investment trajectory produced by the current model is categorically unable to track these dynamics. Investment is therefore excluded from pass-fail assessment, and any investment-related deviation from actual data should be attributed to model architecture rather than calibration quality.

Household wealth proxy. The use of M2 as a proxy for household wealth V materially understates actual household net worth in all five economies, particularly post-2019, when housing wealth appreciation was exceptional across the sample. The resulting understatement of V[−1] in the consumption function biases α₂ upward during OLS estimation (as the OLS algorithm attributes the wealth effect to a smaller base), and may produce over-prediction of consumption in periods of rapid house price growth. This is a data limitation, not a model limitation, but it has model implications.

Interest rate transmission. The current model has no channel from the policy rate rbar to consumption or investment. Central bank rate increases — particularly the 2022–2023 tightening cycle common to all five economies — are therefore modelled only through their effect on banking sector net interest margins, with no real economy transmission. This implies that the model will systematically over-predict GDP and consumption in 2022–2023 relative to actual outcomes for all five economies, since it cannot replicate the demand-destruction effect of rapid rate increases.

Real versus nominal treatment. SIMEX is a real quantity model; all variables are deflated before entry. The deflation methodology uses each country's GDP deflator rebased to 2019. In periods of elevated inflation (2022–2023), the deflator is a large adjustment, and small differences in deflator path produce materially different real series. This means that metrics for the 2022–2023 period are less reliable indicators of model quality than metrics for the 2010–2019 period.

5.3 Implications for Model Development

Regardless of specific results, the validation exercise provides structured information for model development prioritisation. If Y Pearson r exceeds 0.85 for all five economies in the pre-COVID window, this confirms that the demand-driven income-expenditure mechanism at the core of SIMEX is empirically adequate for tracking trend-level GDP dynamics — supporting the model's theoretical foundations. If Y MAPE falls within the pass threshold (<20%) for most economies, this indicates that the calibration methodology produces realistic parameter values and that the initial condition override approach is functioning correctly.

Conversely, if systematic positive bias in Y is observed across multiple economies — consistent with the pattern documented in the New Zealand prototype validation — this would constitute strong evidence that the consumption function parameters require recalibration at the cross-country level, potentially pointing to a specification improvement such as the inclusion of mortgage interest costs in the definition of disposable income.

The cross-country comparison design is particularly valuable for distinguishing universal model properties from country-specific calibration issues. If Y MAPE is elevated for the United States but within threshold for the other four economies, this points to a US-specific calibration issue (possibly in the μ or θ parameter) rather than a fundamental model problem. If Y MAPE is elevated for all five economies in 2020–2023 but within threshold for 2010–2019, this confirms that the COVID structural break is the binding constraint on full-sample performance — a finding that would motivate the addition of a confidence variable or supply-side shock mechanism.

5.4 Broader Methodological Contributions

This exercise makes a methodological contribution independent of its specific results. The explicit pre-registration of pass-fail thresholds, the separation of structural exclusions from calibration failures, the dual pre-COVID / full-sample reporting, and the software framework architecture for repeatable multi-country runs together constitute a template for SFC model validation that the authors believe is absent from the existing literature. The Godley-Lavoie tradition has produced rich theoretical developments; empirical validation methodology has lagged. This paper attempts a partial correction.

The choice of MAPE as the primary accuracy metric is deliberate. MAPE is scale-invariant, which enables meaningful comparison across economies of very different sizes (from New Zealand's approximately NZD 300bn GDP to the United States' approximately USD 21 trillion). The Pearson correlation coefficient is included because MAPE does not distinguish between a model that tracks the trend but has a systematic level offset versus a model that fits the initial level but fails to track subsequent dynamics — both are practically important, and they require different remediation.

6. Conclusion

This paper presents the design and pre-registration of a multi-country empirical validation framework for the SIMEX Stock-Flow Consistent macroeconomic model — a five-sector open-economy extension of the Godley-Lavoie SIM prototype — applied to New Zealand, Australia, Canada, the United Kingdom, and the United States. The framework is, to the authors' knowledge, the first systematic cross-country empirical validation exercise for the Godley-Lavoie SIM/SIMEX model family.

The theoretical framework is grounded in the standard Godley-Lavoie accounting structure, with parameters calibrated by OLS regression (consumption function) and ratio methods (tax rate, import propensity) over the pre-COVID window of 2010–2019. Validation targets GDP (Y), household consumption (C), imports (M), and tax revenue (T), with investment excluded from pass-fail assessment given the structural constraint I = δK in the current model version. Pass-fail thresholds are set ex ante at MAPE ≤ 20%, Pearson r ≥ 0.85, and mean bias ≤ 10%.

What can be concluded at this stage is that the validation framework itself constitutes a reproducible infrastructure for ongoing empirical assessment of SFC models as they are developed. The initial_conditions API capability — the prerequisite for real-economy calibration — is confirmed operational. The calibration methodology produces theoretically consistent parameter values within expected empirical ranges for all five target economies based on published national accounts data. The pass-fail thresholds are set at levels consistent with the standards applied to validated macro models in adjacent traditions.

The aspiration of the project is not merely to validate or falsify the SIMEX model but to establish a running empirical accountability infrastructure: a framework that can be re-run as the model is extended, making the empirical implications of each structural change transparent and testable. Future work will incorporate the investment accelerator, interest rate transmission to consumption, and eventually a price-level mechanism — each of which should, in theory, improve empirical performance on the dimensions where the current specification is structurally constrained. The multi-country framework will allow that improvement to be assessed systematically rather than anecdotally.
