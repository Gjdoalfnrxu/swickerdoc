The Problem

Most models don't enforce accounting

You can build a model where government spending rises, private savings rise, and the current account improves simultaneously — with no sector footing the bill. The numbers look plausible. But somewhere, money appears from nowhere.

SFC modelling closes this gap. The balance sheet constraint is enforced at compile time. If your model violates an accounting identity, you find out before the simulation runs, not after the policy recommendation is made.

⚖️

Balance Sheet Closure

Every financial asset in the model maps to a matching liability across sectors. The compiler validates closure before any simulation runs.

🔄

Stock-Flow Consistency

Flows in each period accumulate into stocks for the next. Deposits, loans, bonds, and reserves evolve coherently through time.

🏦

Endogenous Money

Banks extend loans and create deposits as a result. Money supply emerges from the model's behaviour — not from a quantity theory assumption.

📊

Sectoral Balances

Government deficit, private surplus, and current account balance are constrained by the accounting. Policy analysis respects the identity.

Use Cases

What you can model

The platform is built for researchers, analysts, and anyone who needs macro models that don't handwave the accounting.

Monetary Policy Transmission

Raise the OCR by 150bps. Trace the channel: lending rates rise → investment falls → wages slow → disposable income drops → consumption contracts → tax revenue falls → fiscal deficit widens. Watch each sector's balance sheet evolve.

Fiscal Multiplier Analysis

Does $1 of government spending raise GDP by more or less than $1 in your model? Shock G, measure output response across sectors. Compare deficit-financed against tax-financed spending directly.

Financial Stability Stress Tests

What if firm loan repayments rise as a share of income for three consecutive periods? Model the conditions explicitly, run it forward, and identify when balance sheets become unsustainable.

Scenario Comparison

Run the same model under different parameter assumptions — hawkish central bank vs. dovish, high wage growth vs. suppression — and overlay results on the same chart. Side by side, same accounting constraints.

Open Economy Dynamics

Model the current account, net foreign assets, and exchange rate effects. Show how a country that issues its own currency differs from one that doesn't.

Internet Arguments

"Here is what sectoral balances say about the government running a surplus while the private sector wants to save." Attach the chart. Post it. The accounting speaks for itself.

How It Works

Under the hood

1

Define your model

Specify sectors, variables (stocks and flows), equations like C = alpha1 * YD + alpha2 * V[-1], parameters, and balance sheet entries mapping assets to holder/issuer sector pairs.

2

Compile and validate

The PEG grammar parser tokenises equations, extracts the dependency graph, runs topological sort, and compiles to bytecode for the stack-based VM. Balance sheet closure is validated before any simulation runs.

3

Simulate

Gauss-Seidel iteration solves the system forward period by period until convergence within your tolerance threshold. Apply shocks — level changes, delta changes, percent changes — at any period.

4

Analyse results

Interactive Plotly charts across time series, balance sheet evolution, deviation analysis, and multi-scenario overlay tabs. Export results, compare scenarios side by side, run Monte Carlo sensitivity analysis.

The same engine runs in the browser via WebAssembly — local simulations without hitting the server at all.
