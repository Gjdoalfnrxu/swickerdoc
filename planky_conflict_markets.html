From Ukraine to Hormuz: What the Wars Do to the World Economy
29 MARCH 2026
Not financial advice. Deep research using IMF, World Bank, Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, Oxford Economics, Dallas Fed, and SIPRI data. Current date: 29 March 2026. The Strait of Hormuz has been blockaded since 4 March 2026. Trump's ceasefire deadline expires 6 April 2026.

The Russia-Ukraine war reshaped the global economy. The US-Israel-Iran war and Hormuz blockade is doing it again, faster and broader. Here's the documented template from Ukraine, and what it predicts for the months ahead.

Ukraine: what actually happened to the global economy

Energy shock. Russia supplied approximately 40% of EU pipeline gas before the invasion. Dutch TTF gas hit €200/MWh in August 2022 — from a pre-war baseline of ~€25/MWh. Brent crude averaged $100/barrel across 2022. European coal hit all-time highs. Russia's piped gas exports to OECD Europe fell by roughly 83 billion cubic metres — about 50% — in 2022 alone, its lowest level since the mid-1980s. By 2025, Russia's share of EU gas had collapsed to approximately 6%. Europe spent three years rebuilding its supply chains around LNG, Norwegian gas, and accelerated renewables.

Food shock. Ukraine and Russia together accounted for 36% of global wheat exports and over 50% of global sunflower oil. The FAO Food Price Index hit an all-time nominal high in March 2022. Wheat surged to over $500/tonne by May 2022 — a 40% premium over pre-war levels. Grain prices broadly up 34% year-on-year. Fertiliser prices (nitrogen, potash) up 60–100% as natural-gas-linked production costs exploded. Prices corrected through 2023–24, but in local currency terms, food price inflation above 15% persisted in over a third of low-income and lower-middle-income countries as of late 2023.

GDP and inflation. The IMF quantified the macro hit: global GDP reduced approximately 1.5% relative to the no-war counterfactual; global inflation raised ~1.3 percentage points. 2022 global growth was revised down from 4.4% (January 2022 forecast) to 3.6% (April 2022 actual estimate). Euro area energy inflation averaged 38% through 2022. Every major central bank — Fed, ECB, Bank of England — launched its fastest tightening cycle in decades. The rate shock cascaded into mortgage markets, housing, and emerging-market debt globally. The World Bank estimated the war pushed 27.2 million additional people into poverty and 22.3 million into hunger relative to the no-war baseline.

Defence spending. Global military expenditure hit $2,718 billion in 2024 — a 9.4% real-terms jump in a single year, the steepest since the Cold War. European NATO members and Canada increased spending 50% in real terms between 2022–2025. By 2025, all 32 NATO members met the 2% of GDP target for the first time. The June 2025 Hague summit committed members to 5% of GDP for defence and security by 2035. Defence stocks — Lockheed Martin, RTX, Northrop Grumman, Rheinmetall — gained 50–70% over the 2022–2025 period.

Supply chain fragmentation. Ukraine produced ~50% of global high-purity neon (critical for semiconductor manufacturing lasers), based in Mariupol and Odesa. Russia supplied 40% of global palladium. Both disruptions compounded existing post-COVID semiconductor shortages. The war deepened the structural bifurcation of the global trading order: Russia reoriented exports to China, India, Turkey; Western states accelerated supply chain decoupling from adversary nations.

The Hormuz strait normally carries ~20 million barrels/day of seaborne oil (20% of global supply) and 20% of global LNG trade. Alternative pipeline routes — Saudi East-West pipeline, UAE Habshan-Fujairah — have combined capacity of ~9 million barrels/day. There is no substitute for the missing volume.

Why Hormuz is potentially more severe than Ukraine

The Ukraine war disrupted ~40% of European gas — a regional shock. Hormuz carries 20% of global oil and 20% of global LNG simultaneously. The commodity basket affected is broader: oil, LNG, fertilisers (Gulf produces 50% of world urea, 30% of ammonia), sulfur (~45% of global supply from the Gulf), helium, and aluminium. There is no "alternative supplier" pivot comparable to Europe's LNG diversification — the physical volume cannot be replaced.

Europe enters this shock with gas storage at approximately 30% capacity — unusually low after a hard 2025/26 winter — and economies not yet fully recovered from Ukraine-era energy costs. The Ras Laffan LNG facility damage has a 3–5 year repair timeline regardless of ceasefire. Even in a resolution scenario, the Qatari LNG supply disruption is sticky.

Scenario A: resolution (ceasefire before or shortly after April 6)

Resolution scenario

Allianz base case: Brent spikes to ~$85 peak, ends 2026 around $70/barrel. Equity relief rally 5–10% across S&P 500 and European indices. Gold corrects (already fell 11% in a single week mid-March 2026 on ceasefire rumours — its worst weekly performance since 1983).

Market plays in resolution: Sell the war premium in energy before Strait reopens. Buy beaten-down consumer discretionary, airlines (fuel costs normalise), Asian equities (Nikkei and KOSPI took severe war-open hits — KOSPI crashed 12% on 4 March). Start accumulating European industrials. Reduce safe-haven USD. Begin re-entry into emerging-market bonds from oil-importing countries. US Treasuries: buy as peace premium brings yields down.

Structural persistence even in resolution: Ras Laffan damage (3–5 year repair), elevated insurance and shipping premiums for Middle East routes, Gulf infrastructure repair costs of $25 billion+, European LNG diversification accelerating further. These are not resolved by a ceasefire.

Scenario B: prolonged conflict (April 6 deadline passes)

Prolonged conflict scenario

Goldman Sachs base: Brent averages $110/barrel through Q2. Worst case if Hormuz closed through June: $135/barrel. Theoretical extreme (Iran destroys Gulf production facilities): $200/barrel. Dallas Fed: a two-quarter 20%-supply disruption lowers global real GDP growth by 2.9 annualised percentage points in Q2 2026.

Inflation. Goldman Sachs raises 2026 US inflation by 0.8 percentage points at $98/barrel; 3.3% under $110 scenario. OECD projects US inflation at 4.2% in 2026 under prolonged conflict. UK breaches 5%. Eurozone 2.6–4.4% depending on severity. California gasoline already exceeded $5/gallon by mid-March. Fertiliser prices: if Gulf urea/ammonia disruption persists, nitrogen fertilisers could double from 2024 levels, feeding through to 2026/27 food production costs globally.

GDP and recession risk. Goldman raised US recession probability by 5 percentage points to 25% under moderate disruption. ECB warned prolonged conflict pushes Germany and Italy into technical recession by end-2026. Goldman trimmed 2026 US growth to 2.2%. US 30-year mortgage rate at 6.38% as of 26 March — central banks cannot cut rates into rising inflation.

The asymmetric damage: who gets hurt most

As with Ukraine, the conflict's costs are distributed asymmetrically — falling hardest on countries with no stake in the war and no ability to influence it. The countries most exposed to Hormuz disruption on oil import dependence: Japan (90% of crude imports), South Korea (70%, 95%+ through Hormuz), India (60%), Pakistan. For food: Gulf states importing 80%+ of calories through the Strait are already in grocery emergency. Fertiliser disruption flows through to agricultural producers globally — the 2026/27 Northern Hemisphere planting season faces meaningfully higher input costs.

The Ukraine war's commodity shock pushed 27 million additional people into poverty. The Hormuz disruption, affecting a broader commodity basket across a more connected supply chain, is likely to exceed that number if prolonged through mid-2026.
