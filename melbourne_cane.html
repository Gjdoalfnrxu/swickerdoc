The Case for Melbourne
Cane Edition — labour-protectionist framing, same conclusion
This is not the standard version of this document. This version was commissioned for a hypothetical hardline leftist named Cane, who holds anti-migration views on labour protectionist grounds — the position that high immigration suppresses wages, inflates housing costs, and undermines working class solidarity. This framing is taken seriously and engaged with honestly. The conclusion is still Melbourne. See the original document for the standard analysis.
Disclosure: This document is still advocacy for Melbourne. It simply concedes the migration argument rather than using it as a selling point. Cane's position is internally coherent. It is not Chungus's position. They are different people with different vowels.
01 — Migration, Labour Markets & Housing

The labour-protectionist critique of high migration has a coherent left-wing form: when labour supply increases faster than labour demand, wages in affected sectors face downward pressure. Housing demand increases without corresponding supply increases housing costs for working people. These are real mechanisms, not dog-whistles, and Cane is entitled to weight them.

Melbourne has 37% of residents born overseas. Christchurch has 23%. On Cane's framework, Melbourne is worse on this dimension. This is acknowledged.

Cane's Assessment — Migration Dimension
Melbourne is worse on raw migration numbers. Cane's concern is noted and not dismissed. However: Melbourne's labour movement is stronger, minimum wage protections are higher, and Fair Work Act provisions are more worker-friendly than NZ equivalents. The migration intake is a genuine pressure on housing; the union infrastructure is a genuine counterweight on wages. These partially offset. The migration dimension goes to Christchurch — everything downstream of it is more complicated.

Cane's Note on Housing
Melbourne's housing crisis is, by Cane's own framework, partly attributable to the migration intake he objects to. He is not wrong about the mechanism. Melbourne median house prices are ~$365,000 NZD higher. This is real money. The wage gap must be weighed against it — see section 03.

Finding — Critical for Cane
Even granting the housing premium and the migration concern, Melbourne pays ~$23,500 NZD more per year in take-home. Over five years: ~$117,500. The superannuation gap adds further long-term wealth accumulation. At a senior technical level, Cane is not competing directly with 482 visa holders in significant numbers — the wage suppression concern is real for lower-skilled sectors and some mid-level engineering roles, less so at lead/principal level where local track records and institutional knowledge still command premium. The financial case is strong regardless of immigration views.

Conclusion — Cane Edition
The migration concession is real. Melbourne has more of it, the housing cost reflects it partly, and Cane is entitled to weight that. However: the financial case for Melbourne holds independently of migration views. The wage and superannuation gap is ~$35,000+ NZD/yr combined, which outpaces the cost-of-living premium over any reasonable horizon. Melbourne's labour protections are, ironically, stronger than Christchurch's — the working class is better defended by the Fair Work Act than by the Employment Relations Act. The city Cane would find more congenial for his stated left-wing values — higher minimum wage, better unfair dismissal protection, stronger unions — is Melbourne, not Christchurch. The conclusion is the same. The journey was different.
