Build Economic Models That Actually Balance
3 APRIL 2026

Most economic models lie to you.

Not deliberately — they just don't enforce the accounting. You can build a model where government spending rises, private savings rise, and the current account improves all at once, with no sector footing the bill. The numbers look plausible. The charts look good. But somewhere in there, money is being created from nothing or destroyed into nothing, and nobody in the model knows about it.

Stock-Flow Consistent (SFC) modelling fixes this. Every financial asset has a matching liability. Every flow in the period shows up in next period's stocks. When one sector runs a surplus, another runs a deficit — by construction, not by assumption. The accounting is enforced at compile time.

I built a platform for this. Here's what it does and why it might matter.

What It Is

The SFC Modelling Platform is a web-based tool for building, simulating, and stress-testing macroeconomic models. You define an economy as:

  • Sectors — households, firms, banks, government, central bank, rest of world
  • Variables — GDP, consumption, wages, deposits, loans, bonds, reserves, and whatever else the model needs
  • Equations — the behavioural and accounting relationships that connect them
  • Balance sheet entries — who holds what asset, and who issued it

The engine compiles your model, validates that the balance sheets close, solves the system iteratively, and runs your simulation forward as many periods as you want. If something doesn't balance, you find out before the run, not after.

What You Can Do With It

Policy Analysis

Raise the OCR by 150bps. Watch the transmission: lending rates rise, investment falls, disposable income drops, consumption contracts, fiscal position deteriorates as tax revenue falls.

Fiscal Multipliers

Does $1 of government spending raise GDP by more or less than $1? Shock G upward, measure the output response across sectors. Compare deficit-financed vs. tax-financed.

Financial Stability

What if firm loan repayments rise as a share of income? When do household balance sheets become unsustainable? Model the conditions, run it forward, see where it breaks.

Internet Arguments

"Here is what sectoral balances say about the government running a surplus while the private sector wants to save." Attach chart. Post. Conversation over.

The New Zealand Model

The platform ships with a pre-built five-sector model of the NZ economy:

  • Households — disposable income, consumption, deposit accumulation
  • Firms — GDP, investment accelerator, wage bill, corporate tax, retained earnings
  • Banks — endogenous money creation, interest margin, loan dynamics, reserves
  • Government (Crown) — fiscal balance, bond issuance, tax revenue
  • RBNZ — OCR transmission chain, settlement cash, bond holdings, profit remittance to Crown

All calibrated to NZ institutional structure: ~58% consumption share, ~21% government spending share, OCR + fixed markups determining lending and deposit rates. The model satisfies all balance sheet identities by construction.

How It Works

The backend is Rust. Equations are parsed by a PEG grammar — expressions like C = alpha1 * YD + alpha2 * V[-1] are tokenised, dependency-extracted, topologically sorted, and compiled to bytecode for a stack-based VM. The solver runs Gauss-Seidel iteration until convergence within your tolerance threshold.

The same engine runs in the browser via WebAssembly — local simulations without hitting the server. The frontend is React with real-time WebSocket progress during server-side runs. Results are visualised across multiple tabs: time series, balance sheet evolution, deviation analysis, and multi-scenario overlay comparison.

Who It's For

Researchers and students studying post-Keynesian economics or MMT who want a tool that enforces the accounting they're trying to model. Policy analysts who want to stress-test fiscal positions without maintaining a spreadsheet that probably has an error in column AQ. Anyone who argues about economics on the internet and wants charts that can't be dismissed as "but where does the money come from."

The accounting always closes.
