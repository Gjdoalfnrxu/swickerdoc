What the Numbers Actually Contain

Melbourne's Indian community of ~400,000 people is predominantly permanent residents and citizens — people who have built careers, families, and lives in the city over years and decades. They are not the 482 visa pipeline discussed later in this document. The subset relevant to the structural labour concern (tied-visa IT services workers) is in the tens of thousands across all nationalities — a small fraction of the broader community.

The geographic distribution matters for lived experience. Melbourne's Indian population concentrates heavily in the outer western and northern corridors — Craigieburn, Epping, Dandenong, Point Cook. A technical lead living in inner Melbourne (Fitzroy, Richmond, Collingwood, Hawthorn, South Yarra) would be living in areas where the Indian-heritage proportion is well below the city average. The city-wide percentage and the day-to-day neighbourhood reality diverge significantly by suburb.

The distinction between the community and the structural concern runs through the rest of this document. Most of what follows is about a specific visa mechanism operating in a specific market segment — not about the Indian-heritage population of Melbourne as a whole. If those two things feel like the same thing, the rest of the document is intended to separate them.

Appendix Notes

Sydney comparison — Arab/Middle Eastern: Melbourne's Arab and Middle Eastern community (~1–1.5% of city) is considerably smaller than Sydney's, where Lebanese-Australians concentrate heavily in the western suburbs (Bankstown, Lakemba, Auburn, Merrylands — combined approaching 8–12% of those LGAs). If the mental model of "Arab presence in an Australian city" is built from Sydney's western suburbs, it does not describe Melbourne's reality.

Inner Melbourne diverges from outer-suburb concentrations: The suburbs with the highest concentration of recent migrant communities — Chinese, Indian, Nepalese, Sri Lankan, Sub-Saharan African — are predominantly outer and middle-ring. Inner Melbourne (Fitzroy, Collingwood, Richmond, Ascot Vale, Brunswick, Northcote, South Yarra) has a demographic profile that is substantially Anglo-European with a high proportion of New Zealanders and British. The city-wide percentage does not describe the lived experience of the inner corridor.

Generation matters for social texture: Communities with a high 2nd/3rd generation proportion (Greek, Italian, Vietnamese, Turkish, Lebanese) are structurally integrated — the post-migration adjustment period is two generations past. Communities with high 1st-gen proportions (Indian, Nepalese, Chinese) still contain a significant recently-arrived cohort alongside an established base. This is relevant to social texture, not to any causal labour or crime claim.

01 — Migration, Labour Markets & Housing

The labour-protectionist critique of high migration has a coherent left-wing form: when labour supply increases faster than labour demand, wages in affected sectors face downward pressure. Housing demand increases without corresponding supply increases housing costs for working people. These are real mechanisms, not dog-whistles, and Chungus is entitled to weight them.

Melbourne has 37% of residents born overseas. Christchurch has 23%. On Chungus's framework, Melbourne is worse on this dimension. This is acknowledged.

The Commercial Model Is the Driver — Migration Is the Delivery Mechanism

The analytically honest framing: the visa pathway is downstream of the commercial incentive, not the cause. The actual driver is enterprise procurement optimising for cost at scale. Large clients outsource to firms that can arbitrage global labour. Those firms use tied-visa arrangements because they produce a predictable, captive workforce that reduces delivery risk. Migration is one instrument for achieving this; pure offshoring and remote contracting are others. Remove migration tomorrow and the same commercial logic reroutes through those alternatives. The effect persists because the incentive persists.

Where the empirical pattern concentrates: IT services and government procurement, and more specifically, through India-anchored delivery ecosystems. The major IT outsourcing firms operating this model in Australia — Infosys, TCS, Wipro, HCL and their tier-2 equivalents — are India-headquartered, recruit primarily through Indian graduate pipelines, use established intra-company transfer infrastructure to move workers onto Australian visas, and maintain sponsorship channels that are practically unavailable to workers from other source countries at the same scale. The 482 tied-visa mechanism as it operates in Australian tech services is not evenly distributed across global source countries — it runs predominantly through this India-linked delivery ecosystem. Naming that concentration is not an attribution of cause to the workers: it is identifying where the conduit actually runs. The labour-market effects — downward pressure on bargaining power in those segments, wage floors set by workers with restricted mobility — cluster around Indian workers in those roles because the pipeline is India-centric, not because of any intrinsic property of those workers. Remove the pipeline structure and you remove the effect regardless of the workers' origin. But if you remove the nationality from the analysis you also lose explanatory and predictive power: the mechanism is specifically traceable to how this ecosystem is built and operates.

The pipeline is still a symptom of the commercial incentive; the procurement model that makes low-cost delivery rational is the upstream cause. Reform the visa structure without reforming procurement incentives and the dynamic reappears in a different form. But the correct analytical level for describing where the effect runs in practice is: India-anchored delivery ecosystems as the dominant conduit, structured and used in a way that produces tied-labour conditions at scale in specific market segments.

What this claim doesn't cover: most of Australian tech. The labour market is segmented across axes that matter — commoditised delivery versus product work, labour-substitutable roles versus scarce specialisms, price-driven enterprise contracts versus competitive talent markets. The wage-suppression argument is only coherent in the specific overlap of (labour-substitutable) ∩ (price-driven delivery) ∩ (large enterprise or government client). Outside that overlap — product companies, senior engineering, high-leverage roles — the dynamics are different and the commercial model pressure doesn't apply in the same form.

The implication for Chungus: holding the concern at the migration level is targeting a symptom. The structurally sound version of the concern is: "procurement models that reward lowest-cost delivery create a race to the bottom in commoditised labour segments, and migration is one available instrument." That framing leads to different and more durable policy targets — public contracting standards, insourcing requirements, labour market testing with teeth, portability of tied-visa status — rather than migration volume as such.

Counterweight: Victoria's ICT unions are more active than NZ equivalents; Melbourne's product and specialist sectors are largely insulated from the outsourcing dynamic. Chungus's actual exposure depends on which segment of the market he enters, and at technical lead level he is more likely to be in the product/senior engineering segment where this pressure is weakest.

Chungus's Assessment — Migration Dimension
Melbourne is worse on raw migration numbers. The concern about wage suppression in commoditised delivery segments is real but analytically downstream of the commercial model that produces it. Melbourne's labour movement is stronger, minimum wage protections are higher, and Fair Work Act provisions are more worker-friendly than NZ equivalents. The migration intake is a genuine pressure on housing; the union infrastructure is a genuine counterweight on wages. The migration dimension goes to Christchurch — the structural causes of the concern Chungus is tracking are more complicated, and more of them are addressable in Melbourne than in Christchurch.

Chungus's Concern — Migrant Population & Crime

This concern is taken seriously. The honest answer is that it cuts against the premise.

The robust finding in criminology across Australia, NZ, the UK, and the US is that first-generation immigrants commit less crime per capita than native-born populations, including when controlling for socioeconomic status. This holds across most crime categories. The Victorian Crime Statistics Agency data does not show elevated offending rates in Melbourne's highest-migration suburbs relative to comparable low-migration suburbs at the same income level.

The poverty-crime correlation is real and strong. Some migrant cohorts do experience higher socioeconomic disadvantage — particularly recent arrivals, refugees, and those on temporary visas with restricted work rights. Where elevated offending exists in these groups, it tracks poverty, not migration status. The evidence-based policy conclusion is economic support and settlement services — not reduced migration.

Second-generation immigrants show higher rates than first-generation, converging toward the native-born average. This is a well-documented pattern attributed to the relative deprivation effect — comparing oneself to peers rather than to the country of origin. It is not evidence that migration causes crime; it is evidence that inequality causes crime.

Chungus's concern is noted. The data does not support it as a Melbourne-specific risk. If Chungus genuinely cares about crime reduction, the mechanism is wages and housing — both of which Melbourne provides more of, per section 03.

Conclusion — Chungus Edition
The migration concession is real. Melbourne has more of it, the housing cost reflects it partly, and Chungus is entitled to weight that. However: the financial case for Melbourne holds independently of migration views. The wage and superannuation gap is ~$35,000+ NZD/yr combined, which outpaces the cost-of-living premium over any reasonable horizon. Melbourne's labour protections are, ironically, stronger than Christchurch's — the working class is better defended by the Fair Work Act than by the Employment Relations Act. The city Chungus would find more congenial for his stated left-wing values — higher minimum wage, better unfair dismissal protection, stronger unions — is Melbourne, not Christchurch. The conclusion is the same. The journey was different.
