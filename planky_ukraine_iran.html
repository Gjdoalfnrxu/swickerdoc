Two Wars, One Template: Ukraine and Iran
29 MARCH 2026

When Russia invaded Ukraine in February 2022, it was described as a rupture in the post-Cold War order — unprecedented, shocking, a violation of the norms that had governed great power behaviour since 1945. Four years later, the US and Israel struck Iran, killed its Supreme Leader, and blockaded the Strait of Hormuz. The second rupture happened so fast that people barely paused to note the template was the same.

The parallels between Russia's war on Ukraine and the US-Israel war on Iran are not perfect. The actors are different, the stated justifications differ, the geopolitical alignments differ. But the underlying logic — and the consequences for international order — are nearly identical. Here's the comparison.

The comparison

Dimension Ukraine (Russia, 2022–) Iran (US/Israel, 2025–26)
Justification Security threat from NATO expansion, denazification, protection of Russian-speaking populations. Framed as pre-emptive self-defence. Elimination of Iranian nuclear programme, removal of threat to Israel, degradation of the "Axis of Resistance." Framed as pre-emptive self-defence.
International law Clear violation of UN Charter Article 2(4): prohibition on use of force against territorial integrity of another state. Condemned by UN General Assembly. Same violation. Chatham House: "Trump is making the use of force the new normal and casting aside international law." No meaningful Security Council action possible — US holds veto.
Nuclear dimension Ukraine surrendered its nuclear arsenal in 1994 under the Budapest Memorandum in exchange for security guarantees from Russia, the US, and UK. Russia invaded anyway. The lesson: giving up nukes doesn't buy security. Iran was pursuing nuclear capability. The US and Israel struck to prevent it. The lesson for every other state watching: the only reliable deterrent against regime change is nuclear weapons. North Korea remains untouched.
Miscalculation Russia expected Kyiv to fall in days. It didn't. The war became a grinding multi-year conflict that exposed Russian military limitations. Lavrov noted the US/Israel "miscalculated" Iran's capacity to resist — explicitly drawing the parallel to Russia's own early failures in Ukraine. Iran's deterrence architecture proved more durable than expected; the Strait remains contested.
Economic warfare Sweeping Western sanctions on Russia. Energy markets disrupted. Oil price spike. Russia's war economy sustained by hydrocarbon revenues that proved difficult to fully cut off. Strait of Hormuz blockade: ~20% of global oil supply disrupted. Brent crude at $113–120/barrel — the most severe oil shock since the 1970s. Sanctions architecture against Iran already exhausted; direct military action was the escalation tool.
Proxy and alliance dynamics US and NATO provide arms, intelligence, and financial support to Ukraine without direct military involvement. Russia supplied by North Korea (munitions, troops) and Iran (Shahed drones — before Iran's own collapse). Russia rhetorically supports Iran but provides no meaningful military assistance or escalation cover. Iran's "Axis of Resistance" — Hezbollah, Hamas, Houthis — had been degraded before the strikes. Iran fought largely alone.
Western unity NATO largely unified in support for Ukraine, though cracks emerged over aid volumes, red lines, and burden-sharing. Europe failed to unite. Al Jazeera: "Europe fumbles towards mutual defence" as the US acts unilaterally. The EU Council president noted Russia as the only clear "winner" of the conflict.
UN Security Council Russia blocked Council action via veto. General Assembly resolutions non-binding. US blocks Council action via veto. Same structural paralysis. The institution designed to prevent exactly these conflicts is constitutionally incapable of responding to permanent member aggression.
Impact on the other conflict Iran was supplying Russia with Shahed drones until the US/Israel strikes disrupted Iranian capacity. The fall of Iran has reduced one of Russia's key suppliers. The Iran war has diverted US attention, air defence resources (Patriot interceptors), and political bandwidth from Ukraine. Russia benefits from the distraction. FPRI: "Russia the only winner."

Imperial expansion and broken promises

Both conflicts look different when you place them inside the longer history of imperial ambition and systematically broken agreements. That history makes both aggressors look worse — and makes the pattern harder to dismiss as exceptional.

Russia's imperial logic. Putin's framework for the Ukraine invasion is explicitly restorationist. His 2021 essay "On the Historical Unity of Russians and Ukrainians" denied Ukraine's existence as a distinct nation. His stated aim was to reverse what he called the "greatest geopolitical catastrophe of the 20th century" — the dissolution of the Soviet Union. This is not defensive realism. It is imperial nostalgia translated into military doctrine: the idea that the former Soviet space is rightfully Russia's sphere, that independent states within it are provisional, and that their sovereignty is contingent on alignment with Moscow. Ukraine, Georgia in 2008, Moldova — the pattern predates 2022.

The broken promises to Russia. Russia's grievance over NATO expansion contains a real, if contested, historical core. In 1990, as the Soviet Union negotiated German reunification, US Secretary of State James Baker told Gorbachev that NATO would move "not one inch eastward." Declassified documents from the National Security Archive confirm this assurance was given — along with similar statements from British, French, and German officials — in the context of German reunification. It was never formalised into a treaty. The US position is that the oral assurances were limited to Germany and non-binding. Russia's position is that the spirit of the agreement was broader. Both positions have some documentary basis. What is not in dispute: NATO expanded to include twelve former Warsaw Pact and Soviet states between 1999 and 2004. Whether or not a formal promise was broken, the practical outcome matched Russia's stated red line.

The broken promises to Ukraine. More unambiguous is the Budapest Memorandum of 1994. Ukraine, then holding the world's third-largest nuclear arsenal inherited from the Soviet Union, agreed to give up those weapons in exchange for explicit security assurances from Russia, the United States, and the United Kingdom. The signatories committed to respect Ukraine's sovereignty, territorial integrity, and existing borders. Russia invaded in 2014. It invaded again in 2022. The US, which co-signed the Memorandum, provided military assistance but no Article 5-equivalent defence. The document is not a treaty with enforcement mechanisms — that was the design — but the moral weight of the broken assurance is significant. Ukraine disarmed on the basis of guarantees that proved worthless.

The US and Iran: the JCPOA. The parallel broken promise on the Iran side is the 2015 Joint Comprehensive Plan of Action. Iran agreed to severe restrictions on its nuclear programme — verified by the IAEA, which repeatedly confirmed compliance — in exchange for sanctions relief and an implicit US commitment to the deal's continuity. In 2018, Trump unilaterally withdrew the US from the agreement, reimposed sanctions, and launched "maximum pressure." Iran had held up its end. The US did not. The JCPOA's collapse drove Iran's nuclear programme forward and pushed Tehran further toward Russia and China — creating exactly the strategic environment the deal was designed to prevent. Russia's Lavrov noted at the time that the US was "revising key international agreements" — a criticism that, in light of subsequent events, applies with equal force to Russia itself.

The imperial expansion history. Stepping back further: both the US and Russia have long histories of treating their near-abroad as rightful spheres of influence, enforced through intervention when necessary. The US overthrew the Iranian government in 1953 (Operation Ajax), backing the Shah in exchange for oil access and Cold War alignment. It has intervened militarily across Latin America, the Middle East, and Southeast Asia across decades. Russia has suppressed independence movements in Hungary (1956), Czechoslovakia (1968), Afghanistan (1979), Chechnya (1994, 1999), and Georgia (2008). The Ukraine and Iran conflicts are not aberrations from a peaceful international order. They are the latest expressions of imperial logic that never went away — briefly papered over by the rules-based order rhetoric of the 1990s and 2000s, now openly reasserted.

The uncomfortable symmetry: both Russia and the US have broken formal or informal agreements with states they later attacked or threatened. Both have intervened in smaller states' affairs in the name of security or sphere of influence across decades. Both now lecture the other about respect for international law while conducting or having recently conducted military operations in violation of it. The accusation each makes of the other is accurate. So is the counter-accusation.

The nuclear lesson, stated plainly

The most consequential parallel is the nuclear one, and it runs in both directions. Ukraine gave up its nuclear weapons and was invaded. Iran was building nuclear weapons and was struck to prevent it from completing them. The conclusion that every medium-sized state with security concerns will draw is unambiguous: nuclear weapons are the only reliable deterrent against major power military action. The Budapest Memorandum is dead. The Non-Proliferation Treaty's implicit bargain — give up nukes, receive security guarantees — has been demonstrated to be worthless twice in four years. North Korea, which has nuclear weapons, has not been invaded. The lesson has been taught clearly.

The international order question

Both wars represent the same underlying shift: the post-1945 international order, built on the premise that territorial conquest and unilateral use of force were off the table, has collapsed for states that lack a nuclear deterrent or great power patron. Russia, the US, and Israel have all demonstrated that the UN Charter's prohibition on force is not enforceable against permanent Security Council members or their close allies. Chatham House put it directly: these conflicts "shatter the UN-led global order."

The difference between 2022 and 2026 is that the second rupture happened faster and with less collective outrage. The normalisation Chatham House warned about has occurred. Use of force is now, as they said, "the new normal" — and the absence of meaningful international consequence has confirmed it.

Where they diverge

The parallel has limits. Russia is a revisionist power seeking to restore sphere-of-influence dominance; the US/Israel action was framed as eliminationist — targeting the regime's military and leadership capacity rather than territorial occupation. Russia is bogged down in a grinding conventional war; the US/Israel campaign was a rapid decapitation strike followed by ongoing Strait crisis. Russia faces sustained economic pressure from sanctions; the US faces no comparable external constraint.

The divergence that matters most: Russia's invasion strengthened NATO and European defence spending. The US/Israel action has weakened the Western alliance architecture by demonstrating that the US will act unilaterally without European consultation or consent. Ukraine brought the alliance together. Iran is pulling it apart.

The human cost

The strategic parallels matter. But the most direct way to measure these conflicts is by what they do to people.

Ukraine. By January 2026, the UN Human Rights Monitoring Mission had recorded 56,550 civilian casualties since February 2022: 15,172 killed, 41,378 injured. 2025 was the deadliest year for civilians since the invasion began — 2,514 killed, 12,142 injured, a 31% rise from 2024. Drone strikes alone killed 577 civilians in 2025, up 120% from the year before. Of civilians killed in frontline areas, over 45% were aged 60 or older. 5.86 million Ukrainians are registered as refugees abroad. 3.7 million remain internally displaced inside Ukraine. 12.7 million people require humanitarian assistance — many displaced multiple times. Strikes on energy infrastructure during winter 2025/26 left millions without heating, water, or electricity in sub-zero temperatures.

Iran. By mid-March 2026 — less than three weeks after the US and Israel launched strikes — the NGO HRANA had documented over 3,100 deaths, including at least 1,354 confirmed civilians. On 15 March, a US strike on a school in Minab killed approximately 175 people, most of them children — assessed by human rights organisations as likely the largest single-incident child casualty event in US military history since the My Lai massacre in 1968. More than 884,000 people were displaced from their homes in the first week alone. Iran hosts 750,000 Afghan refugees and 3.65 million Afghan migrants; the war has placed all of them at risk. In the first two weeks of March, over 70,000 Afghan refugees were forcibly returned to Afghanistan, into one of the world's worst pre-existing humanitarian crises. Iran's economy, already contracting under sanctions, is projected to shrink by a further 10%. By early March, 4,000 civilian buildings had been damaged.

The global food and energy shock. The Strait of Hormuz closure on 4 March removed at least 10 million barrels per day from global oil supply. Oil hit $113–120/barrel. Gulf Cooperation Council states — Kuwait, Qatar, the UAE — import over 80% of their caloric intake through the Strait. By mid-March, 70% of the region's food imports were disrupted. The UN Refugee Agency warned that if just 10% of Iran's 85-million population fled, it would rival the largest refugee flows in recent decades. The Strait blockade also triggered a "grocery supply emergency" across the Gulf — a term that would have seemed absurd applied to some of the world's wealthiest states a year ago.

These are not abstractions. Ukraine and Iran together represent tens of millions of people whose daily lives — safety, shelter, heating, food, movement — have been shattered by decisions made in Moscow, Washington, and Jerusalem. The people who made those decisions are not among the displaced.

The honest read

Two permanent UN Security Council members have now launched major wars of choice against smaller states, framed as defensive necessity, in violation of international law, with no meaningful international consequence. The second happened with less global shock than the first. That trajectory — diminishing outrage, normalising unilateral force, the slow death of the collective security framework — is the story of this decade. Both wars are chapters of it.
